Types of Sauna

Traditional Finnish Sauna

The most common sauna type, using a wood stove or electric heater to heat stones. The heat is dry, with temperatures ranging from 70°C to 100°C (158°F to 212°F). Water can be poured on the stones to create steam (known as löyly).

Infrared Sauna

Uses infrared heaters to emit light that is absorbed by the skin, heating the body directly rather than the air. This type operates at lower temperatures (typically between 45°C and 60°C or 113°F to 140°F) and provides a more gentle heat.

Steam Sauna (Turkish Bath or Hammam)

In a steam sauna, water is heated to produce steam that fills the room, creating a humid environment. The temperature is usually lower, around 40°C to 50°C (104°F to 122°F), with high humidity (100%). It’s ideal for improving circulation and skin health.

Wet Sauna

A mix between the traditional sauna and steam room, it involves both heat and steam. It's designed for high humidity but with dry heat. People often pour water on hot stones to increase humidity levels.

Infrared Light Sauna

This sauna uses a combination of infrared light and heat to promote relaxation and healing. It operates at a lower temperature than traditional saunas but provides targeted heat that helps relieve muscle tension and detoxify the body.

Salt Sauna

Similar to a traditional sauna, but with salt-lined walls. The heat combined with salt inhalation is said to promote respiratory health and skin rejuvenation.

Bio Sauna

A hybrid of the Finnish sauna and steam sauna, the bio sauna uses a lower temperature (50°C to 60°C or 122°F to 140°F) and higher humidity than the Finnish sauna. It's often used for a gentler sauna experience.

Russian Banya

A traditional Russian sauna that’s similar to the Finnish sauna but includes the use of a venik (a bundle of twigs) for gentle tapping, which is believed to enhance circulation.

Wooden Sauna

Similar to the Finnish sauna, but often designed for outdoor use. It’s constructed entirely of wood, adding an organic aesthetic to the experience.

Portable Sauna

Smaller and more affordable, portable saunas use infrared or steam technology and are typically made for personal use at home. They can be set up quickly and packed away after use.
